The 3 Essentials of Cartooning

Unlock your drawing potential instantly. Learn the foundational shapes and principles professional cartoonists use every day.

1. Simplification: The Sphere, Cube, & Cylinder

Every great cartoon character, object, or scene starts with the simplest 3D geometry. If you can draw a solid sphere, cube, and cylinder, you can draw anything.

Principle: Building from Form

  • Cartoon Heads: Are almost always based on a Sphere (for the cranium) and sometimes a Cube (for the jaw/muzzle).
  • Arms and Legs: Start as simple Cylinders or slightly tapered cone shapes. Avoid drawing with outlines; draw the forms beneath first.
  • Pro Tip: Think of volume, not lines. Sketch your character lightly using only these three shapes to ensure they feel grounded and can be viewed from any angle.

Line Weight vs. Line Economy

Use Line Economy to focus the viewer. Only use thick lines where shapes overlap, or on the silhouette of the subject (the outside line). Use thin lines for internal details. This reduces visual clutter.

2. The Core Principle: Squash & Stretch

The single most vital principle for injecting life, weight, and personality into your cartoon drawings. This principle makes static objects appear dynamic.

Applying Squash & Stretch

Squash (Impact/Weight)

The shape compresses when hitting a surface or bearing weight.

  • Used for landings, heavy emotions (sadness, fear), or impact.
  • The volume must remain the same: as it squashes down, it must get wider.

Stretch (Speed/Anticipation)

The shape elongates when moving quickly or preparing for an action.

  • Used for jumps, extreme speed lines, and anticipating a movement.
  • The volume must remain the same: as it stretches vertically, it must get thinner horizontally.

The Golden Rule of Volume

Never lose volume. If you squash a sphere to half its height, it must widen to compensate. The goal is to make it look like a rubber ball, not a balloon popping.

3. Exaggeration: If it's Funny, Push It Further

Cartooning is about communication, not realism. Exaggeration is the tool you use to communicate emotion, power, and narrative clearly and instantaneously.

Pushing the Expression

  1. Emotions: If a character is angry, don't just frown. Make the eyes tiny, the brows massive, the head squashed, and the veins pop. Always go past the point of realism.
  2. Action: If a character is running, don't just draw them with legs stretched. Stretch the entire body, lean the silhouette aggressively, and turn the feet into blur streaks (using the stretch principle!).
  3. Proportions: Exaggeration starts in the design phase. If a character is strong, give them a tiny head and giant hands. If they are smart, give them a massive cranium and tiny body. Use contrasts.

Finding Your Style

Your personal cartoon style is simply which foundational shapes you prefer (Round vs. Sharp) combined with how far you are willing to push the exaggeration. Find the work of your favorite artists and analyze their shapes and principles!
