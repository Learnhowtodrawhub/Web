1.1 Choosing Your Surface: The Best Paper for Drawing

Essential Supply Guide for Parents

Understanding Paper Brightness and Color

The paper you choose is the foundation of any great drawing. The most important factor, especially for colored mediums like pencil or marker, is the brightness of the paper.

The Brightness Scale: What the Numbers Mean

Paper brightness is measured on a scale from 1 to 100+, and it indicates how reflective and true white the surface is. Always opt for paper with the highest brightness rating (100+).

Art Mat/Board Recommendation:

For any medium that bleeds (like alcohol markers), you will need a reusable Art Mat or Board. These are typically thin plastic or chipboard, costing between $5 - $15 depending on size, and will last for years.

Different Paper Types and Quantity

Choosing the Right Texture and Weight

For general drawing and sketching, look for Medium Weight Sketch Paper (usually 50-70lb). If your child plans to use ink or light watercolor washes, opt for Mixed Media or Bristol paper, which is heavier (90-100lb) and less likely to buckle.

1.2 Essential Pencils & Erasers

The Core Drawing Tools

Understanding Graphite Hardness (H vs. B)

Pencils come in a variety of hardnesses, measured on a scale from H (Hard) to B (Black/Soft). For beginners, you generally only need a small range. We recommend a starter set containing HB, 2B, and 4B pencils.

The Right Eraser for the Job

  • Kneaded Eraser: Used for lifting graphite subtly.
  • Vinyl/Plastic Eraser: Best for clean, complete removal of dark lines.

1.3 Sketchbook vs. Loose Sheets

Choosing the Right Format for Creativity

The Benefits of the Sketchbook

A sketchbook provides a safe, continuous record of the artist’s progress. It’s perfect for daily practice and portability.

When to Use Loose Sheets

Loose sheets, often higher quality, are best reserved for Finished Art meant for framing or gifting, and are easy to scan or photograph.

2.1 Markers: Choosing the Right Ink

Understanding Alcohol-Based vs. Water-Based Inks

Alcohol Markers vs. Water-Based Markers

Alcohol Markers are for serious coloring (blending, seamless gradients) while Water-Based Markers are best for casual coloring and easy cleanup, though they leave more visible streaks.

Addressing Bleed-Through: The Barrier Method

All liquid inks, especially alcohol markers, will bleed through standard paper. You must use a Barrier Method: always place a thick piece of cardboard, chipboard, or a dedicated Art Mat/Board behind the page you are working on. This protects surfaces and underlying pages.

Marker Brands and Pricing

Marker Type Price Range (24-pk) Example Brands Typical Usage
Water-Based (Student) $8 - $15 Crayola, Prang Young children, casual coloring, easy washability.
Alcohol (Budget/Hobby) $20 - $40 Ohuhu, Caliart Beginner blending, coloring book artists. Excellent value for money.
Alcohol (Professional) $80 - $200+ Copic, Spectrum Noir Professional illustration, design, advanced blending. Often refillable.

Lifespan and Replacement

Water-based markers typically dry out faster (6-12 months of occasional use). Alcohol markers last longer, but their lifespan is determined by how much blending is done. Professional-grade alcohol markers like Copic are designed to be refilled, which is a significant cost saver over time (refills cost about 1/3 the price of a new marker).

2.2 Colored Pencils & Pastels

Dry Color Mediums for Detail and Blending

Understanding Colored Pencil Types

Wax-Based Pencils (softer, better for heavy burnishing) and Oil-Based Pencils (harder, better for fine detail and layering) are the two main types.

Colored Pencil Brands and Pricing

Pencil Grade Price Range (48-pk) Example Brands Core Composition
Student Grade $10 - $20 Crayola, Primsacolor Scholar Hard Wax
Artist Grade (Wax) $50 - $100+ Prismacolor Premier Soft Wax
Artist Grade (Oil) $70 - $150+ Faber-Castell Polychromos, Lyra Oil

Lifespan and Replacement

Colored pencils last a very long time. The only reason to replace them is when they are completely used up or broken. The most frequently used colors (Black, White, Browns) are often sold individually, allowing you to replenish only the colors you need rather than buying a whole new set.

Pastels: Oil vs. Chalk

Oil Pastels are creamy and dense (messy), while Chalk Pastels are dry and blendable (dusty, require a fixative).

2.3 Brushes and Palettes

Essential Painting Accessories

Choosing Brushes: Synthetic is Best

For beginners working with acrylics or watercolors, stick to synthetic brushes. They are durable, affordable, and clean easily. Natural hair brushes are often expensive and require delicate cleaning.

Essential Brush Shapes (Starter Set)

A good beginner kit should include:

  • Round: For lines, details, and filling small areas.
  • Flat (Square-tipped): For broad strokes, blending, and painting straight edges.
  • Filbert (Rounded Flat): For softening edges and blending colors seamlessly.

Palettes and Cleanup

A simple, inexpensive plastic palette is ideal for mixing water-based paints (watercolor, acrylics). For cleanup, always teach your child to rinse the brush thoroughly and never let paint dry near the ferrule (the metal part).

Pricing and Lifespan

A good starter set of 5-8 synthetic brushes costs about $10 - $25 and should last for about a year if cleaned properly. Simple plastic palettes are usually under $5.
