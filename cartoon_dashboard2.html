2.1 The Line of Action (LoA): Capturing Energy

The Line of Action (LoA) is a single, sweeping curve that defines the pose, energy, and flow of your character. It's the first line you draw and the foundation of dynamic drawing.

LoA vs. Center Line

  • Center Line: Divides the form down the middle. It's often static and structural.
  • LoA: Captures the **motion** or **intent** of the character, often running from the head, down the spine, and through a primary limb. It always follows a curve (S, C, or reverse-C shape).
  • The Rule: Never draw a figure with a straight LoA. A straight spine equals a stiff, lifeless character. Even in a standing pose, use a slight S-curve.

Exercise: LoA Exploration

Fill a page with just LoAs. Draw one long, curved line, then draw simple body shapes (spheres/ovals) along that curve. Don't worry about detailsâ€”just capture the *feeling* of a jump, a stretch, or a recoil.

2.2 Defining Character Proportions (The Head Unit)

Cartoon characters aren't built on realistic proportions, they're built on **stylized head units**. The size of the head determines the entire body's length and character type.

The Head Unit System

The height of the head (from crown to chin) is the basic unit of measurement for the whole body.

6-8 Heads Tall

Heroic/Dramatic

Standard adult proportions (e.g., action characters, realistic animation).

3-4 Heads Tall

Toony/Youthful

Exaggerated, cute, or young characters (e.g., classic cartoon mascot).

Key Proportional Breakdown (3 Head System Example)

  • Head Unit 1: The Head and Neck.
  • Head Unit 2: From the shoulders down to the waist/bottom of the torso.
  • Head Unit 3: From the waist down to the feet (legs and feet).
  • The Midpoint: In a 6-head figure, the crotch is the halfway point. In a 3-head figure, the crotch sits much higher, closer to the 1.5-head mark. Stylization is key!

2.3 Building the Block Man (Joints and Connectors)

The **Block Man** is the construction layer you draw *over* the LoA. It replaces the stick figure with solid, 3D forms (spheres and cylinders) placed at joints.

The Joint-to-Joint System

Limb segments should be drawn as cylinders or tapered tubes that connect spheres (joints).

  • Torso: A large, simplified **box or barrel shape** that twists along the LoA.
  • Shoulders/Hips: Always use **spheres** for the shoulders and hips. These spheres indicate the range of motion and where the cylinders of the limbs attach.
  • Forearms/Shins: Draw these as cylinders that **taper**. They should be slightly wider at the top (near the joint) and narrow toward the wrist/ankle.
  • Overlapping: Ensure the spheres of the joints are drawn **overlapping** the cylinders to maintain the illusion of depth from Module 1.

2.4 Executing the 4-View Turnaround (The Consistency Check)

The Character Turnaround is the blueprint. It guarantees that the character will look the same, from the front, side, and back, which is crucial for animation or licensing.

The Grid Technique (Mandatory)

  1. The Base: Draw your **Front View** figure first, establishing the proportions (Lesson 2.2) and a neutral, balanced pose.
  2. Horizontal Guides: Use a ruler to draw horizontal construction lines extending from **every key point** of the Front View: the top of the head, eye line, shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, and feet.
  3. The Side View: Place the Side View next to the Front View. Every joint on the Side View **must align horizontally** with the corresponding line from the Front View. This locks in the character's height and limb lengths.
  4. The 3/4 View: This is the toughest! It shows depth. Draw this figure **freehand** but still use the **horizontal guides** for height consistency. The body parts closest to the viewer should be slightly larger (perspective).

Drawing the Rear View

The Back View should follow the same horizontal guides. Pay special attention to the overlapping curves of the back, shoulders, and rear when rendering the final shapes. This view confirms the 3D volume of the body blocks.

2.5 Weight, Balance, and Contrapposto

A well-posed character feels heavy, not floating. The key to solid posing is understanding how the body compensates for weight shifts.

The Balance Line

Draw a straight, vertical line down from the character's pit of the neck (center of mass). For a balanced pose, this line **must** land between the character's feet. If it falls outside the feet, the character is falling.

The Tilt of Contrapposto

**Contrapposto** is the natural, dynamic shift in the human body when weight is on one leg.

  • When the weight is on one leg, the **hip line** will tilt **up** on the weighted side.
  • To compensate, the **shoulder line** will tilt in the **opposite direction** (down on the weighted side).
  • This opposing tilt creates the beautiful, energetic S-curve through the torsoâ€”a perfect combination of the **LoA** and solid weight distribution.
