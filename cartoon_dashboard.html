Foundations of Cartooning

Module 1: Cartoon Geometry & Dynamic Foundations

From 2D shapes to 3D volume—master the geometry that makes characters solid.

1.1 Setting Up Your Drawing Station

Your environment affects your art. Before we draw a single line, let’s optimize your supplies and posture for long, comfortable, and productive sessions.

Deep Dive: Pencils and Paper

  • Sketching (Light Lines): Use an HB or a hard 2H pencil. These leave light, easily erasable marks for planning and construction.
  • Line Art (Dark Lines): Use a soft 2B or 4B. These create rich, dark lines for the final outline. You can press lightly for construction and harder for line art, all with the same pencil.
  • Paper Type: While printer paper works, using a smooth, heavy-weight bond paper prevents tearing and feels better under the pencil. Consider using a clipboard or drawing board to ensure a flat, stable surface.

Ergonomics and Posture

Drawing is physical labor! Avoid hunching over your page, which causes wrist pain and cramped lines.

  • The Angle: Prop your drawing surface up (use books or a dedicated easel) so it's angled at 30-45 degrees. This is easier on your neck and allows you to see the entire page.
  • Lighting: Use bright, cool-toned light sources to avoid eye strain and ensure you see line contrast accurately.

1.2 Mastering the Cartoon Geometry (2D to 3D)

This is the most critical lesson. Cartoons look flat when you think in 2D shapes. We must switch our minds to 3D forms to create solid characters that can be turned and posed convincingly.

The 2D Foundation & Emotional Archetypes

⬤

Circle / Sphere

Softness, Safety, Youth. (e.g., Mickey Mouse, Babies)

■

Square / Cube

Stability, Strength, Authority. (e.g., Superheroes, Bosses)

▲

Triangle / Cone

Energy, Conflict, Danger. (e.g., Villains, Dynamic Poses)

The 3D Leap: Drawing Volume

To make a Circle look like a Sphere (a ball), you must add minor axes. These are guidelines that wrap around the form.

  • The Equator: The line wrapping horizontally around the middle of the sphere.
  • The Prime Meridian: The line wrapping vertically, usually used as the center line for faces.
  • Curvature is Key: When drawing these lines, remember they must always be curved to follow the shape of the ball. If you draw a straight line across a circle, it flattens it instantly.

Exercise: Overlapping and Grouping

Characters are assembled like mannequins. Draw a simple figure by grouping 3D shapes.

1. Draw a large Sphere (Head). 2. Below it, draw a slightly smaller Cube (Torso). 3. Draw two smaller Spheres for shoulders, making sure they overlap the Cube slightly. 4. Draw two long, overlapping Cylinders for the upper and lower arms.

Notice how the areas where the shapes overlap automatically suggest depth. This is how you build solid anatomy.

1.3 Advanced Warm-Up and Line Confidence

A wobbly line means a shaky idea. Your goal is to draw every line in one fluid stroke. This requires physical practice and learning to draw from the right joints.

The Power of the Elbow and Shoulder

Avoid drawing tiny lines using only your wrist and fingers. This leads to cramped, hesitant, "hairy" lines. Instead, move your entire arm from the elbow or shoulder for larger, sweeping strokes. This generates confidence and smoothness.

Warm-Up Drills (10 Minutes Daily):

  1. The Tapered Line: Draw long, straight lines quickly. Press down hard at the start, quickly lift your pencil off the page near the end, and stop completely. This trains you to control line thickness and speed.
  2. Overlapping Spirals: Draw a large spiral continuously for 30 seconds, maintaining even spacing between the lines. Then, draw another spiral, intentionally overlapping the first. This develops hand-eye coordination for curved forms.
  3. Ghosting Practice: Before drawing a line, move your pencil tip along the path you *intend* to draw, without touching the paper (ghosting). Once you’ve traced the path 2-3 times, commit and draw the final line in one fast, confident stroke. This builds muscle memory.

1.4 The Spherical Head and Placement

The head is our primary tool for storytelling. We use the spherical form from Lesson 1.2 and apply placement lines to turn it into a dynamic cartoon skull, ready for features.

The Basic Construction (Loomis Method Simplified)

  1. The Sphere: Start with a perfect sphere (practice ghosting!).
  2. The Center Line (A): Draw the vertical Prime Meridian, curving it to follow the round surface. This dictates which way the head is facing horizontally.
  3. The Eye Line (B): Draw the horizontal Equator, curving it to follow the round surface. This dictates which way the head is tilting vertically (up or down).
  4. The Jawline: Attach a triangle, oval, or squared shape at the bottom-front of the sphere. This is the jaw and chin. The jawline defines the character's age, weight, and personality.

Action Line and Perspective

The two lines you drew (A and B) must always meet at the same point on the surface of the sphere. This intersection is the nose placement. By adjusting the curve and position of these lines, you can make a character look up, down, left, right, or three-quarters—all from the initial sphere.

1.5 The Anatomy of a Funny Face (The Trifecta of Emotion)

Cartoon expressions are carried by three elements: the eyebrows, the eyes, and the mouth. Master these, and you can draw any emotion imaginable.

1. The Eyebrows: The Emotional Accelerators

Eyebrows are the most powerful tool for conveying emotion, even more than the mouth.

  • Anger/Focus: The brows angle downwards and inward, pushing toward the center. They create shadows over the eyes.
  • Sadness/Worry: The brows angle upwards and inward, creating a 'V' shape where the outer edges droop.
  • Surprise/Shock: The brows arch high above the eye sockets, becoming thin and light.

2. The Eyes: Shape vs. Pupil

In cartooning, the shape of the eye often tells the mood, while the pupil tells the focus.

  • Dot Eyes: Simple dots are best for generic, friendly characters (e.g., Peanuts).
  • Pie-Cut Eyes: An oval or circle with a wedge removed (like Pac-Man) is excellent for intense focus or surprise.
  • Eye Squint: When a character is laughing hard or feeling suspicious, draw the eyes as simple curved lines (squinting) rather than full shapes.

3. The Mouth and Jawline: The Expression Finisher

The mouth follows the lead of the eyebrows, but the shape of the jaw and chin is vital for strong emotional poses.

  • Mouth Position: The mouth should always follow the curvature of the jawline. If the jaw is tilted up, the mouth curves up slightly too.
  • Jaw Drop: For fear or shock, simply draw the mouth shape lower on the head, almost disconnecting it from the rest of the features, and elongate the chin slightly.
  • Subtlety: A simple line with upturned corners conveys a friendly smile; no teeth needed!
